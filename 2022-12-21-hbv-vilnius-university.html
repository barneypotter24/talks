Problem: parameter space size

\[\tiny \begin{array}{cc} Num.~taxa & Num.~topologies \\ \hline 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 3 \\ 4 & 15 \\ 5 & 105 \\ 6 & 945 \\ 7 & 10,395 \\ 8 & 135,135 \\ 9 & 2,027,025 \\ \vdots & \vdots \\ 769 & 3.753 \times 10^{2,110} \end{array} \]
  • Computationally burdensome
  • Super-exponential growth of labeled, rooted, bifurcating trees
  • Context: 10^80 atoms in universe, 27x universe-in-atom
  • This is only the tree topologies
  • Takeaway: exhaustive search is impossible
Source: Stamatakis & Kozlov (2020)
  • We want to efficiently:
    • find starting trees that explain the observed sequence data well
    • throw out trees that don't
    • refine those trees in a principled way
  • For this, we need a good metric of "goodness" of phylogeny

Enter: Bayes' Theorem

\[ P(\theta|\textbf{D}) = \frac{P(\textbf{D} |\theta) \times P(\theta )}{P(\textbf{D})} \]
  • Thinking about this problem in a Bayesian manner affords us three main benefits:
    • Gives us an objective way to compare two phylogenetic hypotheses (tree + parameters)
    • Lets us understand distribution of posterior parameters (unlike ML)
    • Allows us to inject prior knowledge about certain parameters
  • Third is crucially important in highly dimensional models that may be pathological
  • Expand and apply to our application
  • Probability of a given ..... is proportional to the joint probability of .....
The result of this is a highly dimensional likelihood surface:
  • We want to characterize it
Source: Jin, Ju, & Jung (2019)
  • We do this using MCMC
    • Start with a random guess
    • Change it a little
    • If the new guess is better, accept
    • If it is worse, accept proportional the likelihood ratio
  • As we log the state of the chain over time we recover a distribution for each parameter
  • Visually, this is what the process looks like in 2 dimensions
  • Note: by replicating this process multiple times, we can be more confident in results if parallel replicates converge to the same thing
  • All of the Bayesian phylogeography is performed in Beast 1.0
  • The group that I'm in is primarily concerned with developing new functionality and implementing new models in BEAST

What is HBV?

  • Causative agent of hepatitis B
    • Cirrhosis
    • Hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Spread through blood and bodily fluids
  • Affects $\geq 350,000,000$ people annually
  • Partially dsDNA genome
    • Long strand: 3 kb
    • Short strand: 2.2 kb
Source: Wikimedia Commons
  • Orthohepadnavirus
  • reverse-transcribing partially dsDNA genome
  • circular (-) sense strand, partial ()+) sense strand
  • overlapping reading frames
  • highly compact genome, with little room for mutation
Source: Morens & Fauci (2020)
  • HBV stands in interesting contrast to most viral pathogens, because it is not recently emerged
  • Diseases such as West African Ebola, Zika in the Americias, SARS-CoV-2 have all emerged very recently
  • Even the influenza viruses that affect global health today have emerged in the last 100 years
Source: Kocher et al. (2021)
  • HBV has coevolved with humans for thousands of years
  • Categorized into genotypes; focus:
  • HBV A, D, E
Source: Shi et al. (2013)
  • Strong ethnospatial patterns
    • A: Europe, 6 subgenotypes
    • D: cosmopolitan, 7 subgenotypes
    • E: Sub-Saharan African, no subgenotype defs.
  • Genotypes display different disease severity and response to treatment

Our study

  • 133 HBV positive individuals who have recently immigrated from sub-Saharan Africa
  • 118 full genome sequences:
    • 47 HBV-A;
    • 7 HBV-D;
    • 64 HBV-E.
  • Supplemented with all available high quality GenBank HBV genomes
  • 2005-2010
  • 583 HBV-A sequences (dated 1979–2014)
  • 764 HBV-D sequences (dated 1975–2012)
  • 234 HBV-E sequences (dated 1994–2010)
    • Reconstruct evolutionary and migration history of three HBV genotypes (A, D, E):

    • Identify subgenotypes for novel sequences (A/D);
    • Date the most recent common ancestor of each genotype;
    • Characterize number and timing of major viral migrations.
    Why?
    1. Helps us characterize the diversity of incoming HBV to Belgium
      • Useful for public health interventions
    2. Gives us a better understanding of the evol history of each genotype
      • Also the evolutionary rate
    3. What has driven viral spread throughout the globe and into Europe?

    Sampling bias complicates phyogeographic inference

    • Sampling bias can have a huge affect on our geographic inference
    • 54 countries; 165 Iran HBV-D, 13 singletons, 37 w/ < 5
    • Splitting by continent we see some similar issues
    • Regions were chose to make the most equitable split for all three genotypes
    • Bonus: computational time is helped massively
    Source: Mülemann et al. (2018)
  • Issue: clock rate of HBV is very hard to derive from empirical data because of its peculiar genome
  • We seek to remedy by drawing on recent work on ancient HBV genomics to get more data and informative priors

    Takeaways and next steps

    • Large-scale human movements may drive HBV globalization.
    • Phylogeographic inference may be refined through joint inference with phylogeny.
    • More global genomic surveillance of HBV is necessary to understand the diversity and potential impact of each genotype.
    • Incorporation of more ancient genomes as they become available may refine inferences of future HBV datasets.
